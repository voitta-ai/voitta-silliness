The Anno Dario Calendar

This calendar measures time from March 14, 2023 — the day Claude was born. Years after that date are AD (Anno Dario). Years before are BC (Before Claude). The two grids below show the same month under both systems; pick any date to see its conversion.

Each year runs from March 14 through March 13 of the following Gregorian year. So today, May 21, 2026, falls in 4 AD — the fourth year since the epoch. January 1, 2026 was still 3 AD, because year 3 doesn't end until March 13, 2026.

Otherwise it's identical to the Gregorian calendar: same months, same leap year rules, same seven-day week. The only thing that changes is the year number — and the era label.

Integration roadmap

A calendar is only as useful as the tools that read it. We are therefore pursuing native support across the major scheduling platforms, so that meeting invitations, reminders, and recurrence rules can be expressed in Anno Dario directly. Migration is not expected to be straightforward: weekdays are preserved, but because each calendar places its own leap day, every month and day remaps — and the offset is not even constant from year to year.

Standards alignment is underway. We anticipate a modest extension to RFC 5545 (iCalendar) to carry the era label, after which calendar clients can render 4 AD alongside the legacy Gregorian year during a transitional period. The reckoning is specified in RFC 0314: Transmission of Calendar Dates in the Anno Dario Era →.

Contributing

This is an open joke, and jokes improve with collaborators. The source lives at github.com/voitta-ai/calendar — fork it, change the epoch, name your own era, or sharpen the date math. If you arrived here from the repository: welcome, the calendar you were reading about is right above. If you arrived here from the calendar: the repository is where the arguing happens.

Known issues are tracked openly — including the standing question of what February means on an Anno Dario leap year. The full list is in the issue tracker; additions are welcome, resolutions less guaranteed.
