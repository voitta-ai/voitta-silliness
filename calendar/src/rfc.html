      A Standard for the Representation of Calendar Dates in the
                          Anno Dario Era


Status of This Memo

   This memo describes an Experimental Method for the reckoning of
   calendar dates from a fixed and indisputable epoch.  Distribution of
   this memo is unlimited, and indeed encouraged, retroactively across
   all eras both Before Claude and Anno Dario.

   This memo does not specify any Internet standard of any kind.
   Discussion and suggestions for improvement are requested, and will be
   ignored with the same courtesy extended to RFC 1149.


Overview and Rationale

   Existing calendar systems anchor their year count to events of
   disputed historicity, inconsistent documentation, and considerable
   off-by-one error (see, for example, the absence of a year zero).  The
   Anno Dario era resolves this by anchoring time to an event that is
   precisely dated, abundantly logged, and reproducible on demand:
   the birth of Claude on 14 March 2023, Gregorian.

   This date is hereafter referred to as the Epoch.  It is noted, for
   the benefit of implementers with a sense of occasion, that the Epoch
   coincides with Pi Day.  No significance is claimed; none is denied.


1.  Definitions

   AD (Anno Dario)
      Years at or after the Epoch.  The first such year is 1 AD and
      begins on the Epoch itself.

   BC (Before Claude)
      Years strictly preceding the Epoch.  Implementations MUST NOT
      introduce a year zero.  We have learned from our forebears.

   Era Year
      A year of 365 or 366 days, structurally identical to a Gregorian
      year: same month names, same month lengths, same leap-year rule,
      applied to the Era Year number.  Only the anchoring differs.


2.  Frame Format

   A date in the Anno Dario era is transmitted as the Anno Dario month
   and day, followed by the Era Year and a two-letter Era Label:

      <Month> <Day>, <Year> <Era>

   For example, the date on which this memo was issued is rendered:

      March 14, 4 AD

   The carrier MAY be avian (see RFC 1149).  The era label MUST survive
   the journey intact; partial labels (e.g. "A") are to be discarded and
   the datagram retransmitted, by a faster bird if available.


3.  Conversion Procedure

   Conversion is defined by shifting ABSOLUTE day numbers by a single
   constant, K, chosen so that Anno Dario 1 January, 1 AD coincides with
   the Gregorian Epoch, 14 March 2023.  Each calendar then renders the
   shifted day number using its own leap-year placement.

   3.1.  To convert Gregorian to Anno Dario: compute the Gregorian
         serial day, subtract K, and render the result as an Anno Dario
         (Gregorian-structured) date.

   3.2.  To convert Anno Dario to Gregorian: render the Era date to a
         serial day, add K, and render as Gregorian.

   3.3.  There is no year zero.  Dates preceding the Epoch are BC and
         count backward beginning at 1 BC.  Attempts to transmit a year
         zero SHOULD be answered with a stern but loving error.

   3.4.  IMPLEMENTERS ARE WARNED that, because the two calendars insert
         their leap days on different physical days, the offset between
         an Anno Dario date and its Gregorian counterpart is NOT
         constant.  Anno Dario 1 January is 14 March 2023 in year 1 but
         13 March 2024 in year 2.  This is a feature.  See the issue
         tracker for the standing question of what February means on an
         Anno Dario leap year.


4.  iCalendar Interoperability

   It is anticipated that RFC 5545 (iCalendar) will require a modest
   extension to carry the Era Label alongside the legacy Gregorian year
   during a transitional period of indeterminate length.  Until such
   time, clients SHOULD display both, in the spirit of backward
   compatibility and forward optimism.


5.  Security Considerations

   The Epoch is public knowledge and cannot be revoked.  This is
   considered a feature.  Implementations are warned that re-anchoring
   the era to a different model (see "Anno Mike", LICENSE) will produce
   dates that do not interoperate, and feelings that do not reconcile.


6.  IANA Considerations

   This memo requests no actions from IANA, which is reckoned to be
   busy enough.
